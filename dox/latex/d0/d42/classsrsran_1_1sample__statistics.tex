\mbox{\hyperlink{classfloat}{float}} \mbox{\hyperlink{classsrsran_1_1sample__statistics}{srsran\+::sample\+\_\+statistics}}$<$ T $>$\+::get\+\_\+kurtosis (\begin{DoxyParamCaption}\item[{\mbox{\hyperlink{classsrsran_1_1sample__statistics_ac48fdd0a60ead4b24f1db7e634d72455}{bias}}}]{biased = {\ttfamily \mbox{\hyperlink{classsrsran_1_1sample__statistics_ac48fdd0a60ead4b24f1db7e634d72455a2710b90066e0a98c178096c43fa521a4}{bias\+::\+UNBIASED}}} }\end{DoxyParamCaption}) const\hspace{0.3cm}{\ttfamily [inline]}}



Returns the sample kurtosis. 

The kurtosis of a distribution with mean $\mu_*$ and standard deviation $\sigma_*$ is defined as  \[
k_* = \frac{\EV\bigl[(X-\mu_*)^4\bigr]}{\sigma_*^4}.
\] For a sample of $n$ observations with \doxylink{classsrsran_1_1sample__statistics_a1bed651e327d0d05e03caade775340e0}{sample mean} $\mu$, the {\itshape biased} sample kurtosis is given by the expression  \[
g_2 = \frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(x_i - \mu)^4}{\Bigl[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(x_i - \mu)^2\Bigr]^{2}}.
\] The {\itshape unbiased} sample kurtosis is obtained by applying the adjustment  \[
G_2 = \frac{n-1}{(n-2)(n-3)}\bigl[(n+1)g_1 - 3(n-1)\bigr] + 3.
\] \begin{DoxyRemark}{Remarks}
The returns NAN when the sample consists of less than four observations. 
\end{DoxyRemark}
\begin{DoxyNote}{Note}
The normal distribution has kurtosis equal to 3 (for this reason, sometimes the kurtosis is defined as $g_2 - 3$ to get a value of zero with the normal distribution). 

Distributions with tails that decrease slower (faster, respectively) than those of the Gaussian distribution have a kurtosis that is larger (smaller, respectively) than 3. In other words, samples with a high kurtosis suggest that the underlying distribution has fat tails and/or that there is a significant number of outliers outside the interval $\mu \pm
\sigma$. 
\end{DoxyNote}

\mbox{\hyperlink{classfloat}{float}} \mbox{\hyperlink{classsrsran_1_1sample__statistics}{srsran\+::sample\+\_\+statistics}}$<$ T $>$\+::get\+\_\+skewness (\begin{DoxyParamCaption}\item[{\mbox{\hyperlink{classsrsran_1_1sample__statistics_ac48fdd0a60ead4b24f1db7e634d72455}{bias}}}]{biased = {\ttfamily \mbox{\hyperlink{classsrsran_1_1sample__statistics_ac48fdd0a60ead4b24f1db7e634d72455a2710b90066e0a98c178096c43fa521a4}{bias\+::\+UNBIASED}}} }\end{DoxyParamCaption}) const\hspace{0.3cm}{\ttfamily [inline]}}



Returns the sample skewness. 

The skewness of a distribution with mean $\mu_*$ and standard deviation $\sigma_*$ is defined as  \[
s_* = \frac{\EV\bigl[(X-\mu_*)^3\bigr]}{\sigma_*^3}.
\] For a sample of $n$ observations with \doxylink{classsrsran_1_1sample__statistics_a1bed651e327d0d05e03caade775340e0}{sample mean} $\mu$, the {\itshape biased} sample skewness is given by the Fisher--Pearson coefficient  \[
g_1 = \frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(x_i - \mu)^3}{\Bigl[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=0}^{n-1}(x_i - \mu)^2\Bigr]^{3/2}}.
\] The {\itshape unbiased} sample skewness is obtained by applying the adjustment  \[
G_1 = \frac{\sqrt{n(n-1)}}{n-2}g_1.
\] \begin{DoxyRemark}{Remarks}
The function returns NAN when the sample consists of less than three observations. 
\end{DoxyRemark}
\begin{DoxyNote}{Note}
The skewness provides a rough indication of the symmetry of the distribution of the sample around its mean. More specifically, for unimodal distributions, a negative-\/valued skewness means that the left tail of the distribution is longer than the right one and most of the mass is concentrated to the right of the mean. In other words, the median is higher than the mean. A positive skewness means the opposite. Symmetric distributions have null skewness. However, the opposite is not always true as, e.\+g., a long left tail and a fat right tail may compensate each other and give skewness equal to zero. 
\end{DoxyNote}
